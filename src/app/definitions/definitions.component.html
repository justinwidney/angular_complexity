Economic Complexity Index (ECI)

The Economic Complexity Index (ECI) measures the diversity and sophistication of a country's or region's export basket. It reflects the knowledge and capabilities embedded in the economy.
Key Characteristics:
  • Higher ECI: Indicates more complex, knowledge-intensive exports
  • Lower ECI: Suggests reliance on simpler, resource-based exports
  • Relative Measure: Countries are ranked relative to each other
  • Predictive Power: Higher ECI is associated with faster economic growth
Calculation: ECI is calculated using an iterative process that considers both the diversity of exports and the ubiquity of those exports. It's derived from the Method of Reflections, which balances:
  • The number of products a country exports competitively
  • The complexity of those products (measured by how few countries export them)
Interpretation:
ECI > 1 High complexity economy with sophisticated export capabilities
ECI: -1 to 1 Moderate complexity with mixed export portfolio
ECI < -1 Lower complexity, often resource-dependent economy
Product Complexity Index (PCI)

The Product Complexity Index (PCI) measures how complex or sophisticated a particular product is, based on the capabilities required to produce it competitively.
Key Characteristics:
  • Capability Requirements: Higher PCI products require more sophisticated capabilities
  • Export Exclusivity: Complex products are exported by fewer countries
  • Knowledge Intensity: High PCI indicates technology and skill requirements
  • Market Access: Complex products often have higher barriers to entry
Examples by Complexity Level:
High Complexity (PCI > 1)
  • Pharmaceuticals
  • Precision instruments
  • Advanced machinery
  • Electronic components
Medium Complexity (PCI: -1 to 1)
  • Textiles
  • Basic chemicals
  • Processed foods
  • Simple machinery
Low Complexity (PCI < -1)
  • Raw materials
  • Agricultural products
  • Basic minerals
  • Petroleum products
Revealed Comparative Advantage (RCA)

Revealed Comparative Advantage (RCA) measures whether a country or region has a comparative advantage in exporting a particular product, relative to the global average.
Formula:
RCA = (Xij / Xit) / (Xwj / Xwt) Where: Xij = exports of product j from country i, Xit = total exports from country i, Xwj = world exports of product j, Xwt = total world exports

RCA < 1 No Comparative Advantage: The region exports this product less intensively than the world average
Practical Applications:
  • Trade Policy: Identify products where a region has competitive strengths
  • Economic Development: Target products aligned with existing capabilities
  • Investment Decisions: Guide resource allocation toward competitive sectors
  • Market Analysis: Understand relative positioning in global markets
Distance

Distance measures how far a product is from a country's current export basket in terms of required capabilities. It indicates the feasibility of developing exports in that product.
Key Concepts:
  • Capability Similarity: Products requiring similar capabilities are "closer"
  • Development Feasibility: Lower distance suggests easier market entry
  • Strategic Planning: Helps identify logical next steps for export diversification
  • Path Dependency: Economic development follows predictable patterns
Distance Levels:
Close Distance (< 0.5) Product requires capabilities similar to current exports - relatively easy to develop
Medium Distance (0.5 - 1.0) Moderate capability stretch - possible with focused development
Far Distance (> 1.0) Requires significantly different capabilities - challenging to develop
Strategic Implications:
  • Low Distance + High Complexity: Ideal opportunities for economic upgrading
  • High Distance + High Complexity: Requires substantial capability building
  • Low Distance + Low Complexity: Easy wins but limited growth potential
Frontier

The Frontier represents the optimal set of products that maximize both complexity and feasibility - products that are sophisticated yet achievable given current capabilities.
Frontier Characteristics:
  • Pareto Optimal: No product offers both higher complexity and lower distance
  • Strategic Focus: Represents the best opportunities for economic development
  • Dynamic: Changes as capabilities evolve and new products emerge
  • Policy Relevant: Guides industrial policy and investment priorities
Frontier Analysis:
High Complexity, Low Distance Prime Opportunities: Sophisticated products within reach
High Complexity, High Distance Long-term Targets: Require significant capability development
Low Complexity, Low Distance Quick Wins: Easy to develop but limited impact
Low Complexity, High Distance Avoid: Neither feasible nor valuable
Product Space

The Product Space is a network visualization that maps the relationships between different products based on the capabilities required to produce them. Connected products require similar capabilities.
Network Structure:
  • Nodes: Individual products or product categories
  • Edges: Connections indicating capability similarity
  • Clusters: Groups of related products (e.g., chemicals, machinery)
  • Density: Indicates how interconnected product capabilities are
Key Insights:
  • Dense Areas: Products with many connections offer more diversification opportunities
  • Sparse Areas: Products with few connections may be dead ends
  • Pathways: Connected products show potential development paths
  • Positioning: Current exports determine future opportunities

Additional Concepts
Opportunity Gain The potential ECI increase from developing exports in a particular product
Ubiquity Number of countries that export a product competitively - lower ubiquity indicates higher complexity
Diversity Number of products a country exports competitively - higher diversity can indicate more capabilities
Feasible Products Products that are close to current capabilities and could be developed with reasonable effort
